My Journey So Far

The Coca-Cola Company

Group Director, Core Technologies

I had the rare opportunity to lead the team responsible for making Coca-Cola Freestyle dispensers pour over 200 drinks from one machine. In my eight years with Coke, I grew the team from 7 engineers to a team of over 50 people, and their talent with software, fluid dynamics, hardware, and electronics became the envy of the industry.

We co-created 10 different new dispenser offerings, lowered cost of those dispensers by over 25%, improved reliability by 10% year over year, and consistently rolled out new drinks to delight our customers. I also had the opportunity to travel all over the world, from Bangkok to Rome and to represent a loved brand. What an amazing experience!

Tracker Marine

New Product Development Director

For a brief period of time, I had the privilege of leading product development for all the boats made by Tracker Marine. My co-workers were some of the most helpful and kindest people you could wish for. My days as often as not included time in the shop and on the lake. I really enjoyed the work we did.

I could have stayed with Tracker forever, but sometimes life has other plans, and the opportunity to design the future of Coke's Freestyle beverage platforms was just too good to pass up.

VP, Engineering

Lindsey recruited me out of Fluke, as they needed someone to look after the engineering function for their largest division, Irrigation.

While there, I had the chance to learn a whole new business, and to lead a team that literally reinvented the wheel to change a business dynamic.

Fluke Biomedical

Director of New Product Development

When I joined Fluke, it was a Danaher company, and I was part of the fastest growing division in Fluke, Fluke Biomedical. This was a time of intense learning for me, as I learned more about how to manage a business from the Danaher Business System training and our day-to-day operations than I had learned to that point. Additionally, this was my first foray into the medical device space, with 510(k) pre-market approval regulation, along with European medical device and machinery directives a central part of our design strategy.

I continued my pattern of partnering with the manufacturing team, made some great friends, and created more best-in-class products.

This was another role where I could easily have spent years, as the technical cutting-edge culture of innovation, along with the accountability of Danaher, resonated so strongly with my personal beliefs. In the end, though, I had a great career opportunity come along, and it fit better with what my family needed at that time.

Club Car

VP, Engineering

When Ingersoll-Rand bought Trane, it opened the door to opportunities across the corporation, and my work at Trane had opened a couple of doors. One of those led to my first truly senior leadership position, leading engineering at Club Car.

Trane

Trane was a fantastic next step in my career. I was leading 60 people to design our flagship commercial rooftop air conditioner, and I had no direct authority over the team. If it was an engineering challenge, I owned it. Manufacturing? I'm the guy. Service? Finance? Marketing? It's all my responsibility! Fortunately, the whole organization had a common mission and the family-like atmosphere helped me build influence quickly, resulting in our shipping our first unit two days ahead of schedule. Our biggest challenge after that milestone was keeping up with demand!

I went on at Trane to become a functional manager, with direct responsibility for all the engineering team working on all of our large commercial products. We had a regulatory deadline that was a massive challenge for the team, yet that challenge became a burning platform and focus, and we were able to get it all done in advance of the regulation.

Probably the most memorable time I had at Trane was in a large review meeting with our division president, Craig. At one point, it was clear that one of the teams had sold a customer a piece of equipment that was not meeting the need, and after several iterations of trying to retrofit it, Craig asked the team what it would take to buy our competitor's unit and install it in place of our unit on the customer's roof. When I think about customer obsession, that conversation is an anchor point for me, and ended up being foundational in my business beliefs.

Visteon Corporation

When Ford spun off its partsmaking organization into a new company, Visteon, it was a new opportunity to learn. Like Ford, Visteon had a strong philosophy that to design products well, partnering with the manufacturing facility was key, and my experience in manufacturing shaped how I think about product design to this day. Unfortunately, the company was not well-positioned financially, and after multiple rounds of watching friends get laid off, I decided to search for a new role, which led me to a great experience at Trane.

Ford Motor Company

I learned engineering in school, but I learned how to be an engineer at Ford. The combination of extreme quality expectations, highly complex products, fierce competition, and the resulting pressure on cost taught me efficiency of design. Learning to ask if every penny I spend on the product is something a customer would pay for was a graduate degree in itself.

Ford also afforded me the opportunity to learn many skills, as I worked in Product Planning, Design Engineering, Manufacturing Engineering, Strategy, Marketing, Program Management, and even IT. I met some amazing people and made some lifelong friendss.

And yes, I did actually get to do just a little bit of work on the Aston-Martin DB7.

University of Illinois

College of Business

I returned to Illinois to pursue my MBA. Over the years, many people have questioned this decision, but at the end of the day, it was the right one for me.

I ended up paying my way through business school writing expert system software for a professor's side business, and eventually managing two other software developers. I'm grateful that Professor Kolstad gave me this chance, for I developed key skills in both technical and people management.

I looked at the world through a new lens, I learned some new skills, earned my MBA, and most importantly, I got to spend more time with a lady that had suffered through a long distance relationship with me and hadn't given up. As a sid note, we've been married now for over three decades.

Stanford University

Electrical Engineering

If there is a Disneyland for engineers, it might just be at Stanford. While the coursework was tough, the facilities are amazing, the faculty is brilliant, and the lifelong friendships I made are incredible.

While there, I learned to scuba dive, windsurf, and sail. I also saw some pretty famous people just talking to students on the campus, among them the Dalai Lama. For a farmtown kid from Illinois, it was a whole new world, one I loved to explore.

Between extracurriculars, I did manage to complete two graduate degrees in electrical engineering before deciding not to pursue my PhD. At that point, it was time to move on.

University of Illinois

College of Engineering

Having grown up in a small town near Peoria, and having been bored through most of high school, college was a whole new world, and I threw myself into it.

I worked harder than I had in my life, made some great friends, and had some great experiences. In retrospect, I probably should have done more social stuff, as I probably would have had more fun, but I had set a goal to graduate in three years, and I wanted to hit that goal.

My Beliefs

What I Have Learned On The Journey

Assume Positive Intent

Listen to understand. Create a safe environment. Collaborate. Focus on the issues.

Bias for Action

Problems do not get better with age. Opportunities have a short shelf-life.

Run the Experiment

Working prototypes beat closely-held opinions.

Strong opinions loosely held

Opinions form from what we know and what we think. When new learnings change either, form a new opinion.

Love Thy Customer
And every other neighbor

Treat your customer like you want to be treated. Figure out how you can make them love you. Then do more of whatever that is.

Life is a Team Sport

Choose your team well, and treat them well. Invest in your team, including yourself.

Be an Owner

Take accountability for results. Deliver your promises. Make the organization stronger for tomorrow's challenges.

Learn From Mistakes

View Failure as the tuition for learning. Don't pay tuition twice for the same lesson.

Show People What Great Looks Like

Share the vision. Show the path. Help wherever you can. Coach each other.

Simplify

Make everything easier to understand and use

Be Endlessly Curious

Learn every day. Curiousity is the fountain from which innovation springs.

Have Fun

As Poor Richard would say, time is the stuff of which life is made. Enjoy what you do.
